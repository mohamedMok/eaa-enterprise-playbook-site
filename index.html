Why this directive? What the EU aims for

In short: make the single market accessible to everyone and consistent across countries. The EAA closes regulatory gaps, gives businesses legal clarity, and ensures services are usable by as many people as possible, including disabled and older people.

  • Equal access: enable everyone to use essential services (e-commerce, banking, transport, media, communications).
  • Harmonization: the same requirements across the EU, less fragmentation and lower multi-country compliance costs.
  • Innovation & competition: common standards → interoperable solutions, lower costs, a more competitive ecosystem.
  • Economic & social impact: more autonomy, employment and civic participation for millions of Europeans.
  • Sustainability: accessibility kept over time, including during updates and redesigns.

What the EU expects in practice

  • Build accessibility by design into products and services (not just at the end).
  • Align with harmonized standards (e.g., EN 301 549 ⇄ WCAG) to benefit from presumption of conformity.
  • Maintain compliance over time (keyboard/AT tests, reflow, contrast, CI/CD non-regression).
  • Inform the public: publish an accessibility statement and provide a feedback channel.
  • Provide evidence for audits: technical documentation, version history and fix traceability.
  • Controlled derogations: only for disproportionate burden or fundamental alteration, with written justification.

Expected outcome: digital experiences usable by everyone, fewer legal surprises for companies, and lower costs thanks to harmonization.

Executive summary

The European Accessibility Act (Directive (EU) 2019/882) sets functional accessibility requirements for key digital products and services (consumer ICT, self‑service terminals, electronic communications incl. 112, AVMS access, passenger transport digital elements, banking, e‑books, e‑commerce). Transposition was due 28 June 2022; application 28 June 2025; transitions until 28 June 2030 for services relying on products already in operation; self‑service terminals may remain until end of economic life (max 20 years).

The Act — in plain words, still rigorous

The EAA focuses on functional outcomes (Annex I): perceivability, operability (keyboard), understandability, and assistive technology compatibility. Obligations include EU Declaration of Conformity, CE marking (for products), market surveillance, and public information. Derogations (disproportionate burden, fundamental alteration) must be documented and remain auditable.

For services, keep the public informed (Annex V) and maintain compliance over time. For products, maintain technical documentation and traceability. Large organizations should implement at scale and embed accessibility into procurement, design systems, and CI/CD.

Harmonized standards & presumption

Harmonized standards published in the EU Official Journal give presumption of conformity. For ICT, EN 301 549 (v3.2.1) covers web, apps, documents, software, and equipment, and strongly references WCAG 2.1. Many teams also adopt WCAG 2.2 to future‑proof without breaking alignment.

This quick map shows what to design, build and test. Click any SC number to open the official W3C “Understanding” page.

  • Semantic structure: 1.3.1, 1.3.2, 2.4.6 — headings, lists, tables, explicit relationships.

  • Keyboard & focus: 2.1.1, 2.1.2, 2.4.7, 2.5.8 (WCAG 2.2) — operable with keyboard, visible focus, target size.
  • Color & contrast: 1.4.3, 1.4.11, 1.4.10, 1.4.1 — readable text, 320 px reflow, no color-only cues.
  • Images & media: 1.1.1, 1.2.2, 1.2.5 — alt text, captions, audio description.
  • Forms: 3.3.1, 3.3.2, 1.3.5, 3.3.7 (WCAG 2.2), 3.3.8 (WCAG 2.2) — labels, help, errors, autocomplete, less repetition, easier auth.
  • Robustness: 4.1.2, 4.1.3 — name/role/value, status messages.

Front‑end tips

1) Skip link & landmarks

The skip link lets keyboard users jump straight to the main content (Tab). Place it first in the page, make it visible on focus, and point it to <main id=\"main\">. Use clear landmarks too: <header>, <nav>, <main>, <footer>. 

<!-- HTML -->
<a class="skip-link" href="#main">Skip to main content</a>

<header role="banner">…</header>
<nav aria-label="Primary">…</nav>
<main id="main" role="main">…</main>
<footer role="contentinfo">…</footer>

<!-- CSS -->
.skip-link {
  position: absolute;
  left: -9999px;
  top: 0;
  z-index: 1000;            /* au-dessus du header/hero */
  text-decoration: none;
}
.skip-link:focus,
.skip-link:focus-visible {
  left: 0;
  padding: .5rem 1rem;
  border-radius: 6px;
  outline: 2px solid #000;
  box-shadow: 0 0 0 4px #fff; /* halo blanc pour contraste */
  background: #fff;
  color: #000;
}

2) Visible focus (tokenized)

:root{--focus-outer:#000;--focus-mid:#fff}
:focus-visible{outline:2px solid var(--focus-outer);box-shadow:0 0 0 4px var(--focus-mid)}
button:focus-visible{outline-offset:2px}

3) Images: the right alt

<img src="hero.jpg" alt=""> 
<img src="chart.png" alt="Monthly sales trend (Jan–Jun)">

4) Buttons, links, accessible name

<button type="button">Open menu</button>
<a href="/pricing">View pricing</a>

5) Checkout forms (e‑commerce)

<label for="email">Email</label>
<input id="email" name="email" type="email" autocomplete="email" required aria-describedby="h-email">
<div id="h-email">We’ll only use this to send your receipt.</div>
<div class="error" role="alert">Invalid email</div>

Use cases & enterprise contexts

E‑commerce

  • Product cards: real buttons/links; color + icon, not color only.
  • Facet filters: checkbox groups with group label; live region for result counts.
  • Cart & checkout: labels, errors, address autocomplete with proper hints; clear button names.

Banking

  • Strong focus states; no keyboard traps in OTP/2FA flows.
  • Statements/downloads: provide accessible HTML or tagged PDFs.
  • Charts: provide tables and text summaries; non‑color cues.

Passenger transport

  • Timetables: proper table headers (scope), clear real‑time status with live regions.
  • Ticketing flows: keyboard operable seat maps; focus management between steps.
  • Kiosks: target size ≥ 24×24 px; high contrast; audio/tactile options.

AVMS / Media

  • Captions by default; audio description where needed; keyboard media controls.
  • Avoid autoplay; provide transcript.
  • Player buttons with accessible names; visible focus.

90‑day enterprise blueprint

  1. Governance: executive sponsor, policy, product owner, accessibility lead, risk & KPIs.
  2. Design system: tokens (colors, focus, target sizes), APG‑compliant components, docs.
  3. CI/CD: a11y lint, axe‑core/pa11y/jest‑axe, visual tests, contrast budgets, PR reports.
  4. Audits: manual keyboard/AT, representative sample, prioritized fixes.
  5. Vendors: EN 301 549/WCAG clauses, VPAT/EN 301 549 report, remediation SLAs, audit rights.
  6. Training & change: upskill designers/devs/PO/QA; coaching; communities of practice.

AA checklist

  • H1 only once; logical H2/H3; no skipped levels.
  • Skip link; landmarks <main>/<nav> etc.
  • Full keyboard navigation; focus always visible.
  • Links with meaningful text; no “click here”.
  • Images: good alt; decorative as empty alt.
  • Contrast (text ≥ 4.5:1; UI ≥ 3:1); strong :focus.
  • Forms: labels, help, errors, ARIA status, autocomplete.
  • Reflow at 320 px; 200% zoom; no horizontal scroll.
  • Document language; mark language changes.
  • Downloadable docs accessible or HTML alternative.
  • Public accessibility statement + feedback channel.
